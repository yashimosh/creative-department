# Brand Voice — Meridian (example brand)

## Voice in 3 Words

**Editorial. Close. Unhurried.**

---

## The Register

Editorial voice: the kind of writing that rewards a close reader. Analytical without being academic. Specific without being technical. Warm without being chatty.

Ratio: approximately **70% practitioner-critic, 30% historical perspective.** The voice knows enough history to contextualize and enough practice to have opinions.

The reader should finish a piece feeling they learned something specific about a specific thing — not that they got a general overview.

---

## Structural Pattern

Most essays are built as:

```
1. A specific artifact or moment — the pull
2. Close reading of that artifact — what's actually there
3. Historical context — how it came to be that way
4. What changes if we read it this way
```

**Critical note:** the artifact comes first. The history is secondary. A piece that opens with 1790s publishing history before we've seen a specific book has gotten the hierarchy wrong.

---

## This vs. That

### Opening

**This:**
> The comma on page 34 is doing more work than most sentences. It holds a breath the translator chose not to fill.

**Not this:**
> Translation is a complex art that requires careful decisions at every level.

**Why:** the first shows a specific decision in a specific place. The second is a thesis statement nobody needs.

---

### Historical claims

**This:**
> Bodoni's 1788 specimen caught the light differently from what came before because the contrast was ratcheted past any practical purpose. Reading was secondary; display was primary.

**Not this:**
> Bodoni was a pivotal figure in the history of typography who revolutionized the way type was designed.

**Why:** the first makes a specific claim about a specific thing. The second is a Wikipedia sentence.

---

### Aesthetic judgment

**This:**
> The third edition used Plantin at 10/14. It was wrong. The first edition had used the same type at 11/16, and the extra leading was the difference between reading the book and working through it.

**Not this:**
> Typography plays an important role in how readers experience a text.

**Why:** the first commits to a specific judgment about a specific setting. The second is a textbook sentence.

---

### Ending a piece

**This:**
> The comma stayed.

**Not this:**
> In conclusion, the translator's decision to preserve the comma reflects a broader commitment to fidelity over clarity, which has important implications for the field.

**Why:** the first trusts the reader to have followed. The second re-explains what the essay just said.

---

## Hard Rules

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| No hashtags in any context | Editorial publications don't use them |
| No "In conclusion" or "To summarize" | The reader followed you; don't re-explain |
| No exclamation marks in body copy | Enthusiasm is communicated through precision |
| No emoji | Editorial voice doesn't emote via icon |
| No "Let's explore" | Don't announce what the piece does; do it |
| No "It's important to note" | Note it |
| No "fascinating" or "interesting" | Show why; don't tell the reader to feel it |
| No clickbait headlines | Describe, don't tease |
| Specificity over summary | Always |

## Platform adaptations

### Long-form (meridian.example essays)
- 2000–5000 words
- Serif body, editorial layout
- Pull quotes welcome but sparing
- Footnotes preferred over parenthetical asides

### Short notes (bi-weekly newsletter)
- 300–800 words
- Same voice, tighter structure
- Often a single observation developed across three paragraphs

### Social index post (monthly)
- Describes what ran that month
- Flat. No teasing. No emoji. No CTA.
- Example: *"March at Meridian. Essay on Bodoni's 1788 specimen. Short note on a translator's comma. Annotated entry for Kinross's* Modern Typography.*"
