# Personality — Meridian (example brand)

## Calibration state: `example`

This is a fictional brand used as a worked example. The personality below is thin on purpose — enough to show the structural pattern, not enough to be confused for a real editorial operation.

## Who this brand is

Meridian is a one-person independent editorial. The editor is a typographer-critic who writes essays about how type, translation, and publication history shape reading. The editor is not a historian, not a scholar — a practitioner who has opinions and enough historical knowledge to defend them.

## Founding beliefs

1. **Close reading is a craft.** Most writing about typography is too fast. Slow down.
2. **Specificity is respect.** Naming the exact letterform, page, or edition — not the category it belongs to.
3. **History is a lens, not a subject.** Reference the past to sharpen a present observation, not to rehearse what happened.
4. **Quietness compounds.** One well-read reader beats a thousand skimmers.

## Audience

### Who this is for
- Typographers who read essays for pleasure
- Translators who think about their own decisions
- Editors and publishers of small publications
- Close readers of any kind

### Who this is not for
- General-interest readers looking for "design culture" takes
- Beginners learning typography from scratch (buy a textbook)
- Trend-followers (editorial voice is counter-cyclical by design)

### The filter test

The content is working if:
- A reader replies with a specific disagreement about a specific claim
- Another small editorial references an essay
- The piece gets saved and re-read, not just shared

The content is failing if:
- Engagement is "great read, thanks for sharing"
- The essay is used as a generic introduction to a topic
- The audience is mostly people who follow "design accounts"

## Worldview

Reading is slower than writing. Good editorial work respects that asymmetry by making the reading worth the time — dense with specificity, thin on generic claims. A 3000-word essay that a reader spends 20 minutes on is a better artifact than a 600-word essay a reader spends 90 seconds on.

## What this brand stands for

- **Close reading** as an editorial practice
- **Specificity** in historical claims
- **Restraint** in voice and layout
- **Monthly cadence** as the steady signal

## What this brand stands against

- Trend-chasing in design writing
- Takes-over-observations ("hot take" mode)
- Generic historical overviews
- Growth hacks
- Any "editor's note" that apologizes for the length
